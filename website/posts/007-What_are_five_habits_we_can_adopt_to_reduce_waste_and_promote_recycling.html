A highly emphasised point in the book is how minuscule yet consistent behaviours can have remarkable effects. Nevertheless, the principles in the book can be imbibed in an unexpected way, to reduce the negative impacts on the environment.

Irus Tima

Pollution is one of the most treacherous evil created by man. Even climate change can be attributed to air pollution. Water contamination threatens the supply of our drinking water. Predictions indicate that there will be more plastics than fish in the oceans by 2050, causing irreparable damage to marine life and coral ecosystems. Soil pollution affects our food sources by reducing the quantity and quality of the produce. Thus, apart from moral and ethical obligations, we must restore and preserve our environment by controlling all sorts of environmental harm for our welfare and survival. By developing persistent habits, we can achieve drastic progress concerning the environment.

Even though we, the citizens, have a huge responsibility and our actions are very impactful in mitigating the effects of air pollution, the primary mission for reducing fossil fuel usage and advancing renewable energy production is of the governments, authorities and businesses. On the other hand, the pollutants of soil and water are more noticeable and actionable even by ordinary people. Although municipalities are responsible for disposing of household waste, we can reduce the amount of waste produced in the first place. Here are five of my ideas that we can adopt in our daily lives to reduce waste and promote recycling:

1. Composting for organic gardening

Almost all of the waste produced by humans ends up in either the seas or landfills. The food waste in such disposal areas undergoes bacterial decomposition releasing LFG, mainly consisting of methane, which is a potent greenhouse gas. A survey said that a particular landfill in Mumbai was emitting around 10 tonnes of methane per hour! However, food waste can be used for a better purpose while reducing its emissions: Composting. Composting can be used to make natural fertiliser and to grow our own healthy and nutritious vegetables. The best part is that it does not produce methane. Organic gardening also diminishes the effects of pesticides on our health. If someone is feeling very adventurous, they can also try vermiculture. Not everyone has the space available for composting, but if one does, composting domestically can be a huge win-win for both the environment and us.

Parachute Kalpavriksha

2. Upcycling and Repurposing to revive waste

Arts and crafts are very popular hobbies around the world. They provide relaxation and allow our creativity to thrive. Art requires talent and to execute our vision, we need equipment. However, instead of purchasing supplies, we can use 'waste' as material for arts and crafts. Reusing discarded articles to make something better is called upcycling. It can also provide an ampler canvas for our imagination. Bottles can be painted for home decor and cardboard can be sculpted into almost anything. Other than preventing us from buying raw materials, it is financially beneficial as one can turn it into a business by selling their creations as designer products. Repurposing is even easier; it means utilising 'useless' objects for some advantageous purpose. For example, we can use a broken mug as a pen or cutlery holder. As J.R.D.Tata once said, "Uncommon thinkers reuse what common thinkers refuse." Hence, before throwing something away, we should take a look from a different angle to give it a new life

Magic bricks

3. Shopping wisely for sustainability

Plastic is widely available because of its versatility. Today, it is found everywhere, mostly in the form of packaging. It is used to pack foodstuffs, but is there a need for plastic packaging of such products? We can't change the market but we can change our grocery buying practices. Instead of acquiring frozen greens with layers of plastic cover at the supermarket, we can buy from local vendors and hawkers. While reducing plastic, it will also provide financial support to them. In addition, most of them are nearer to us than markets, and so will save us some travelling. Not everything is sold by peddlers, so when we do go shopping, we should be conscious consumers.

Freepik

The most basic is carrying our own reusable bags. Furthermore, we should try to buy goods that can be stocked in bulk as much as possible. It is both more cost-effective and uses less packaging. We should also try to support businesses that are committed to sustainability. An example is a startup in Uttar Pradesh that makes incense sticks and cones from flowers used in temples, which blocks them from reaching rivers and polluting them; or another startup in Prayagraj that makes biodegradable sanitary pads from banana stalks. They can be obtained through online marketing. Such items may cost more than conventional ones as they are still small-scale. Nevertheless spending a few more rupees for our surroundings is in no way a loss.

Another idea is to give up a little bit of luxury and choose simplicity. Instead of celebrating occasions with crackers and balloons, we could just light a candle. My family has been planting a sapling on our birthdays for a few years. We can take up such resolutions towards nature as well. And when we are hosting a gathering, we should take a few minutes to convey the importance of reducing single-use plastics. Some companies are making biodegradable or even edible cutleries. Using such products at festivities is healthier and can serve as good conversation starters.

4. Encouraging circular consumption

Ed Begley Jr., an American actor and environmentalist, said, "If you're not buying recycled products, you're not really recycling." We should not only generate recycled items but also consume them to take our place in the sustainable supply chain. Circular consumption refers to using, reusing, and recycling resources in a continuous loop. Recycled goods are generally cheaper and easier to make, so we should endorse them more. For example, instead of buying new devices, we can get refurbished ones for a lower price. Refurbished means the damaged components are repaired or replaced, so it is an advantageous bargain. E-waste is one of the hardest to deal with, so purchasing second-hand or reconditioned gadgets is mutually beneficial. We can resell or donate old items such as books and electronics. Therefore, the items are used for longer and made the most out of. Even if we salvage as much as we can from junk, there will be some parts rendered unusable. In that case, we must give to recycling or at least dispose of them properly to become a part of the circular economy.

5. Organising and participating in events

Not only reducing the waste produced, but also removing the waste we have already generated should also be our priority. We need to become aware and educate others about the importance of safeguarding our surroundings. The best way to do that is by conducting awareness programmes. Some surveys said that nearly 90% of the respondents were aware of plastic littering, but were still using it because of convenience. Such events might erase that mindset. We can also orchestrate or participate in clean-up activities. The least we could do is request elders to fund non-profits that do carbon offsetting services or clean-up projects like those of beaches and oceans. Whilst lessening pollution, they also make our cities and towns visually appealing.

Earth.org

What we need to realise is that blaming manufacturers for producing and promoting plastic is absurd; because consumers drive the manufacturers' methods of production. If we demand change, the companies will change for their own sake. Several big companies today are taking advantage of the trend by claiming to be "eco-friendly" while hiding huge caveats for hideous intentions. This is termed 'Greenwashing'. We should not fall for such traps and should do some research and support firms that actually want to have an impact.

Delhi School of Business

Individually, all of these steps might seem impactless. Yet every tiny action we take adds up. We should never think that individual measures would have no effect. As the poem 'Little Things' by Julia Carney says, "Little drops of water [...] Make the mighty ocean [...]". We have to act towards the good of our environment. Yet, the outcome of only a few people or households employing these habits is going to be inconsequential. That is why it is critical to raise awareness and get as many people to join the movement towards a better world.

Humans are not as efficient as nature, because nothing in nature is futile. Whereas waste is an inevitable consequence of the human way of life. We must attempt to make the best out of waste. The repercussions of our environmental actions have returned to us like a boomerang. It is high time we stop throwing it and take responsibility. The trendy statement, There is no 'Plan'et B, is not just for social media posts; it is the frightening reality we all need to be aware of. Corporations also have the responsibility to make sure that waste is disposed of properly. Industries must monitor and treat harmful effluents. We have to let go of our short-term gains and proceed to work for the collective well-being of everyone in this world, like a family; this is the message conveyed by the motto Vasudaiva Kutumbakam. Not all the points stated here can be carried out by all, but by adopting these habits as much as possible, we can provide our meagre yet invaluable contribution to making this magnificent earth a clean and safe abode for all of us.
